K-Means Clustering for Water Treatment Planning

Unsupervised Learning · Clustering · PCA · Feature Scaling · Domain Interpretation

Python Pandas scikit-learn K-Means PCA Model Evaluation

Designed an unsupervised learning workflow to cluster water bodies using measured quality attributes with the goal of exploring whether clusters could support tailored treatment plant strategies.

GitHub Repo

Problem Statement

Water quality varies significantly across sources due to different contaminants and environmental conditions. If water bodies can be clustered into meaningful groups, treatment strategies could be standardized and adapted for new samples based on the cluster they belong to (e.g., treatments targeting nitrates vs metal content).

The goal of this project was to test whether clustering could identify actionable groupings of water samples that would meaningfully support treatment planning.

Data

The dataset included quantitative water quality attributes describing each sample (e.g., chemical composition and contaminant measures).

  • Inputs: water quality feature vectors (numeric attributes)
  • Preprocessing: handling missing values, scaling features, converting to binary/thresholded representations where applicable
  • Goal: cluster samples and interpret clusters as potential treatment categories

Approach

I implemented a clustering pipeline and evaluated variants with and without dimensionality reduction to assess stability and interpretability.

  • Feature preprocessing and scaling to standardize attribute ranges
  • Applied K-Means clustering across candidate cluster counts
  • Evaluated clustering quality using internal metrics (e.g., silhouette score) and practical interpretability
  • Applied PCA to reduce dimensionality and improve clustering performance/visualization

Evaluation & Findings

Best Variant PCA-reduced binary dataset performed best across evaluated metrics.
Optimal Clusters K-Means selected too few clusters (k = 2), limiting actionable segmentation.
Conclusion Clustering was not viable for treatment design under this dataset structure.
  • PCA-reduced versions consistently outperformed non-PCA variants on clustering metrics
  • Optimal k was too small to support practical treatment differentiation
  • Results highlighted the importance of domain knowledge: computational “best” does not always equal meaningful

Interpretation

Although clustering metrics suggested k=2 as optimal, this outcome implied the dataset did not naturally form distinct, actionable groups. From a treatment planning perspective, it is not meaningful to design curated treatment plants if the algorithm effectively recommends “treat everything” across most samples.

This project demonstrates the critical step of validating machine learning outputs against real-world feasibility and economics, rather than relying on metrics alone.

Challenges & Limitations

  • K-Means assumes spherical clusters and equal variance, which may not match real water quality distributions
  • Binary thresholding can simplify signals but may remove important nuance
  • Unsupervised “ground truth” is ambiguous — interpretability and domain constraints matter

Extensions & Future Work

  • Try alternative clustering methods (GMM, hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN) that support non-spherical clusters
  • Incorporate domain-informed feature weighting (e.g., prioritize regulated contaminants)
  • Evaluate cluster usefulness via treatment cost models or expert-labeled categories
  • Explore semi-supervised approaches if limited labeled treatment classes exist

Key Takeaways

This project strengthened my ability to build and evaluate unsupervised learning pipelines, compare preprocessing strategies (binary vs continuous, PCA vs non-PCA), and interpret results through a practical, real-world lens. It reinforced that strong modeling requires both statistical rigor and domain validation.
